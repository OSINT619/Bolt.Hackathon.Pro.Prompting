Sectioning

Breaking your prompt into clear, focused sections isn't just helpful—it's essential. Here's why:

1. Token Limits

Tools like Bolt have strict token caps (typically between 4k–32k). Feeding the model smaller, more targeted sections prevents cutoff issues and ensures your full input is processed.

2. Increased Accuracy

Isolated sections keep the AI focused, reducing confusion and improving precision in output. One section at a time means clearer intent and better results.

3. Faster Debugging & Iteration

When something breaks, it's way easier to pinpoint the problem in a single section than wade through a monolithic prompt.

4. Tokens Go Fast

Complex projects = more tokens = more credits. Don't waste money OR free credits. Structure your prompts to get high-quality results efficiently. (This approach has helped me create many sucessful business models.)

5. Don't Skip the Research

Know your performance requirements, data structures, compliance needs, storage solutions, etc. If you don't, ask the AI. Compare with similar apps. Learn before you build. Laziness here will cost you later.

To Keep Things Running Smoothly:

  • Briefly reference earlier sections (e.g., "see Section 0 for context…").
  • Store reusable elements (like branding guidelines or roles) in a shared doc or Bolt's .bolt/prompt memory.
  • Ignore the hype—"1 sentence = full app" sounds great, but it leads to shallow results. You're aiming for quality.

Choose the method that matches your task. Don't be afraid to mix and match.

1. Zero-Shot Prompting

Definition: Ask the model a question with no examples.

Example: "Translate 'The weather is nice today' into French."

Best For: Quick tasks with clear objectives.

2. Few-Shot Prompting

Definition: Provide a few examples to guide the model.

Example:
"Translate:

1. 'Hello' → 'Bonjour'

2. 'I love books' → 'J'aime les livres'

3. 'The weather is nice today' →"

Best For: Pattern recognition and consistent formatting.

3. Chain-of-Thought Prompting (CoT)

Definition: Prompt the model to reason step-by-step.

Example: "John has 3 apples, buys 2, gives 1 away. How many left? Let's think step by step."

Best For: Logic, math, and reasoning.

4. Meta Prompting

Definition: Ask the model to create a prompt.

Example: "Write a prompt to summarize a scientific article for a 10-year-old."

Best For: Prompt engineering and AI-assisted instructions.

5. Self-Consistency Decoding

Definition: Generate multiple answers, then select the most consistent one.

Example: "A train travels 60 miles in 1.5 hours. What's its average speed? Think step-by-step."

Best For: Tasks requiring high accuracy and logical validation.

6. Generate Knowledge Prompting

Definition: Ask the AI to recall relevant facts before answering.

Example: "List facts before answering: Why do birds fly south for winter?"

Best For: Research-heavy or knowledge-based tasks.

7. Prompt Chaining

Definition: Use output from one prompt as input to another.

Example:
Prompt 1: "Summarize this article."

Prompt 2: "Generate questions based on the summary."

Best For: Multi-step workflows like writing or planning.

8. Tree of Thoughts (ToT)

Definition: Explore multiple reasoning paths like a decision tree.

Example: "You're solving a puzzle. At each step, consider all options and show your reasoning tree."

Best For: Strategic thinking and planning.

9. Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)

Definition: Pull in relevant external data before generating a response.

Example: "Based on these documents, what are aspirin's side effects?"

Best For: Factual accuracy and document-based Q&A.

10. Directional Stimulus Prompting

Definition: Guide output using tone or emotional cues.

Example: "Write a poem about the ocean with a mysterious, awe-inspiring tone."

Best For: Creative writing, marketing, emotional tone control.

11. Graph Prompting

Definition: Use structured data (like a graph) as input.

Example: "Given this ecosystem graph, explain the energy flow."

Best For: Technical tasks involving relationships or data structures.

12. ReAct Prompting (Reasoning + Acting)

Definition: Combines reasoning steps (thoughts) with actions (like API calls or searches), allowing the model to think, act, observe, and repeat.

Example:
"You're an assistant solving a question.
Thought: I should look up the definition of 'entropy'.
Action: Search('define entropy')
Observation: Entropy is the measure of disorder in a system.
Answer: Entropy is a measure of disorder…"

Best For: Tool-augmented AI agents, research workflows, or systems that combine logic and tool use in a looped reasoning process.
